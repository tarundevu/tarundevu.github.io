LED Cricket Bails

A few years back I used to play cricket frequently. The area we played at had very low lighting. To make it easier to tell when a player was out(and also because they look cool), I decided to LED zing bails for my wickets. My first design involved a plastic container as the body and 3D printed spigots. The electronics consist of a 12V battery holder, 12V LED strips, 2 reed switches, a 2N2222 NPN transistor, a 1000ohm resistor and a SPDT slideswitch. The basic working principle is as such, there are magnets embedded into the stumps, when any of the reed switches detect the magnet, the LED will be OFF and only when BOTH reed switches are away from the magnet will the LEDs light up.

Circuit

This is the schematic for the bails. The positive side of the battery is connected to the LEDs and a 1K ohm resistor which in turn is connected to the base of the transistor. The LEDs are then connected to the collector of the transistor. The reed switches are connected in parallel to each other between the 1K ohm resistor and ground.

Bail Body

This is the body for the bails. The main enclosure is made from a plastic container while the spigots are 3D printed.

Electronics

Above shown are the front and back views of the circuit assembly. The entire circuit is encased around the battery holder. The whole circuit can be removed to change the battery or for repairs.

Improvements

During testing, it works fine but I noticed that it is not very robust, the spigots are starting to come loose and the battery tends to get dislodged from the holder due to the impact from falling onto the ground. The issue is that the plastic body is made from polypropylene which is known to resist chemical reactions, thus, I cannot glue it and instead depend on mechanical fastening. I believe this can be mitigated by either by using a solid body which means it has to be custom made or find a container that is made from a different type of plastic.
