LED Cricket Bails V2

Improving on the previous iteration of my LED bails, I came up with a new design that would be more durable. To recap, the issues faced were the inability to bond polypropylene and the unprotected circuitry. To tackle this, I thought maybe custom building the bails would be suitable. I first thought of 3D printing, however, I was unable to find shops that printed transparent PLA, the closest I could find is PETG. Unfortunately, After I printed it and drop tested it(Yes, it was painful to watch), the longer spigot tends to break off. I realized this did not occur with PLA. So, I thought of the next best option which is epoxy resin. Casting resin is a very messy and time consuming process but I continued with it as I had no other option. As for the circuitry, I designed a 3D printed holder with a suspension-like feature that would hopefully reduce the impact from falls and protect the circuit. Also, I replaced the reed switches with the A3144 hall sensors.

Circuit

This is the schematic for the imporoved bails. The positive side of the battery is connected to the LEDs and two 10K ohm pullup resistors which in turn are connected to the bases of the transistors. The LEDs are then connected to the collector of the first transistor and the collector of the second transistor is connected to the emitter of the first. The hall sensors are connected to power, ground and each of the transistors. Unlike the old design, this one uses two transistors that act as an AND gate with the hall sensors as input.

Bail Body

This is the new body, it has been casted from epoxy resin. I first 3D printed the bails and casted them in silicone to create a mold. Then I injected epoxy resin into the mold to create the bail's body.

Electronics

These are the 3D printed parts that make up the circuitry holder. It is designed to be easily printed and assembled using just glue. The circular parts are the "suspension" that would help to keep the circuitry from making contact with the body.

Above shown are the front and back views of the circuit assembly. The sensors now have a dedicated holder.

Improvements

After casting the bails, I found out that they are soft, I thought they would be much harder. I re-casted them thinking that I might have not mixed the resin properly or messed up the ratio. They came out better but still soft for my standards. I decided to test it out and see if it functions. After testing, the spigot broke off again. I am unsure of why the casted parts were not hard as when I have casted trial items from the same batch, they were perfect. Perhaps it has to do with my trial items being exposed to air so the bubbles escaped easily. I might have to explore other methods to build the body.
